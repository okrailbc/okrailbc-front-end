## What is Valley Rail?

Valley Rail is a hydrogen-powered, electric engine passenger tram-train that we want to bring to the Thompson-Okanagan region. This train would connect over 420,000 people from Osoyoos to Kamloops and every city in between via the Highway 97 corridor. We want to make it easier for people to travel between cities by giving them another choice.

## What are the benefits of a Valley Rail?

Valley Rail would connect our communities, improve our quality of life and protect our world-famous environment.

Benefits include:

- Reduced traffic congestion
- Less noise/pollution from cars
- Improved road safety with less need for winter driving
- Easier access to health care, e.g. helping people reach Kelowna General Hospital for cancer treatments
- Safer wildfire evacuation due to more reliable escape routes
- More options for people who can’t or choose not to drive, like seniors, youth, families with young children, people with medical conditions and others
- Seniors who no longer drive can age in place
- Improved access to affordable housing by having a shorter commute to and from work
- More local investment and job opportunities because companies will have an easier time recruiting and retaining new employees
- Improved airport connections
- Visitors can reach smaller communities without adding to congestion.

Hydrail is zero-emission technology, so it helps us meet our climate action, sustainability, economic development and reconciliation goals. Similar rail projects have reduced traffic by a third or half because people can choose the train instead of being forced to drive everywhere.

We estimate the value of these benefits to the community at $2.7 billion per year.

## What is hydrail?

Hydrail is short for “hydrogen fuel cell/battery hybrid rail power,” meaning an engine that’s either partially or fully powered by a hydrogen fuel cell.  It’s zero emission and high efficiency with affordable on-board electric power, which makes it ideal over long distances.

Hydrogen is one of several ways to power the train’s electric motors. Others include diesel, batteries and overhead wires. The problem is there are 340 kilometres between Osoyoos and Kamloops, and a lot of the terrain is hilly and mountainous.

- **Overhead electric wire:** 340 km is a long distance to string overhead wires along, which would effectively double the costs.
- **Batteries** would run out of power before the train reached the end of the route. Hydrogen fuel cells prevent having to stop the train to recharge along the way.
- **Diesel** emits fossil fuels when it burns, which worsens air quality and makes wildfires more likely.

We have a lot of natural resources to create green hydrogen, space for solar panels and other green options to generate power. Green hydrogen is an eco-friendly solution that works with our natural landscape rather than against it—a good fit for our community.

## What is a tram-train?

A tram-train is a hybrid train designed for both city and rural use. It can climb any highway in any season anywhere in Canada while keeping up with or exceeding regular car traffic.

Within city boundaries, a tram-train acts like a Toronto streetcar (aka tram), capable of making tight corners and stopping on a dime. The rails can embed into the pavement so cars can share lanes and cross over them.

Outside city boundaries the tram-train acts like a light rail train, capable of speeds up to 150 km/h (subject to highway speed limits).

In higher-speed zones, tram-trains can run along the road median or beside the highway, allowing them to bypass other road traffic.

 
Tram-trains are a proven concept that have been around for over 40 years in Germany. When the German city of Karlsruhe started using tram-trains, [ridership quadrupled](https://www.karlsruher-modell.de/en/index.html) compared to previous transit methods, in part because passengers saved transfer time between vehicles. That’s the change we want to bring to our inter-city bus systems.

Tram-trains are about the length and weight of a freight truck (e.g. Rocky-Mountain-double). Unlike heavier freight rail, tram-trains are light enough to operate on asphalt roads and other infrastructure designed for cars. This means tram-trains can run in mixed traffic alongside existing cars, which makes them cheaper to build due to lower property-acquisition costs.

## Why hydrogen fuel cells instead of batteries?

The 340 kilometres separating Osoyoos and Kamloops are a long route to run solely off battery power. Getting the appropriate batteries would be difficult and expensive, and the train would need to stop in the middle of the route to recharge.

To make trains cheaper and more energy efficient, we plan to use a combination of batteries and made-in-Canada hydrogen fuel cells. Hydrogen fuel cells are lighter than batteries, which makes them cheaper and easier to carry up and down hills. Hydrogen can be easily converted into electricity during travel.

## Why not just buses instead? 

A few reasons:

- **Tram-trains are safer.** The Thompson-Okanagan region gets snowy and icy in the winter. Tram-trains travel on rails, which prevent them from losing traction and sliding off the road like cars and buses can.
- **Tram-trains are more eco-friendly.** Tram-trains don’t have rubber tires like buses. Tires degrade quickly and pollute the environment with microplastics as well as drive up costs due to the need to replace them.
- **Tram-trains are more cost efficient for long inter-city trips.** Tram-train vehicles last upwards of 30 years, whereas buses last around 10. Tram-trains have fewer parts to maintain and replace than buses, which makes them cheaper to maintain, especially for routes that don’t change.

  Buses are a great tool for cities to provide a flexible, cost-effective service to their ever-changing communities. But we’re not just building for today’s riders—we’re building for decades to come. Highway 97 isn’t going anywhere, so we can confidently invest in it by putting down tracks along its path.

  When it comes to long-term costs, there’s no contest—the tram-train is more economical.

- **Tram-trains are more comfortable.** Tram-trains have wider carriage bodies and more versatile services. This means more seat space and room for amenities like food, child play areas and bicycle storage for long-distance trips. Studies have shown people (prefer to use a comfy train over a bus)[link here](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1077291X22007391). A wider carriage makes it easier for people to navigate with wheelchairs, walkers, etc.

## Will these trains work in our cold climate and on hills?

Yes. Hydrail trains can go anywhere city buses can, including up and down hills and through snow and ice. This train service would be safer than existing bus services in bad weather since trains have rails to keep them from skidding on ice.

  No time to read a technical paper but you’d like a little more info?  Here's a summary: UBC researchers analyzed a tram–train service inspired by the Karlsruhe, Germany model but powered by zero-emission hydrogen fuel cell/battery hybrid rail power (hydrail) technology. Single-train simulations confirmed its technical feasibility, showing a roughly 8 hour roundtrip travel time between Osoyoos and Kamloops at an average velocity of 86 km/h, including on steep Highway 97 grades and in mountainous weather. Five tons of water per day would be consumed over 16 hours daily by the tram–train-vehicle fleet. This technology disruptor has the potential to become a project of national significance and could improve access to our north coast.

These results were risk-tested, including worst-case risks such as 70 per cent cost overruns. Valley Rail promises an economic transition to clean energy, sustainable transportation, and more livable communities, benefiting all Valley communities through greater transportation equity.

## How can this be economically feasible?

Because we need a solution to worsening traffic in the Thompson-Okanagan. The train we’re proposing is more economical than the alternatives, which include expensive options like widening the highway or building a second bridge.

According to the study Dr. Lovegrove published in 2023, this train would bring $40 billion CAD of benefits to the region. That’s an estimated nine times more benefit than it would cost to build.

While we acknowledge it would be costly to build and operate, it’s the most fiscally and environmentally responsible way to get tens of thousands more people to their destinations in the coming decades.

## Would this be disruptive to businesses and homes during construction?

The goal is to minimize disruption as much as possible. The train would run on existing highway right-of-way, so most homes and businesses along the route shouldn’t be affected. The one exception might be the train stations themselves. Community planners and partners would determine these locations to best serve the community and minimize impacts on property owners. There’s no need for drilling or tunnels.

Construction would be no more disruptive than road improvements. Widening Highway 97 would be far more disruptive. 

## Do we have the population density for this project?

Yes. Forty years ago in Karlsruhe, Germany, where tram-trains were launched, the city was the same size as Kelowna and German cities and rural areas of similar density were connected. If they made it happen, so can we.

The Thompson-Okanagan region comprises an estimated 530,000 people. Many are concentrated in metropolitan areas like Kelowna (~220,000) and Kamloops (118,000), but hundreds of thousands more live in cities along the highway—Penticton, Vernon, Salmon Arm, Oliver, Osoyoos, Lake Country and others.

Because these cities are already built along highway roads, ready to be connected, one train line running along the highway would serve an estimated 80 per cent of the region’s population -- over 420,000 people.
