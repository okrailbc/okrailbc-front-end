## Background

[According to the Thompson-Okanagan Tourism Association](https://www.totabc.org/about/region), the Thompson-Okanagan region covers approximately 71,600 square kilometres, roughly the same size as Ireland. This diverse area with 9 sub-regions is home to over 120 communities, including 33 Indigenous communities.

[BC Stats](https://bcstats.shinyapps.io/popApp/) estimates that the total population of the four regional districts in the Thompson-Okanagan reached **592,997** as of July 1st, 2023. [That's expected to increase](https://www.castanet.net/news/Kelowna/469943/Thompson-Okanagan-population-projected-to-explode-over-next-20-years) to **832,000+ over the next 20 years.** For perspective, Victoria's metropolitan area has 397,000 people.

Of these 590,000+ people, [census data shows](https://censusmapper.ca/) that about 80% of them live in municipalities and metropolitan areas along Highway 97—for example, the Kamloops metropolitan area and Kelowna metropolitan area, as well as places like Salmon Arm, Armstrong, Vernon, Summerland, Penticton, Oliver, and Osoyoos.

The places where people live also tend to be the places where people want to visit—for school, work, medical care, vacation, and so much more. The Kelowna General Hospital and YLW international airport serve people from this entire region, and VIA Rail connects Kamloops to all of eastern Canada.

## The problem

Because the Thompson-Okanagan region is so big, these major city centers are all far apart. [According to Google maps, it would take a staggering 35 hours](<https://www.google.com/maps/dir/Kelowna+International+Airport+(YLW),+Airport+Way,+Kelowna,+BC,+Canada/Kamloops,+BC,+Canada/>) for someone to walk from Kamloops to Kelowna! That's not practical on a day-to-day basis. That's why we need transportation solutions.

[According to KelownaCapNews](https://www.kelownacapnews.com/news/shocking-statistics-show-just-how-car-centric-kelowna-has-become-3167602), right now, people in Kelowna alone use 124 million parking spaces per year, and they collectively drive to the moon and back twice per weekday. Many users on the [Kelowna subreddit](https://www.reddit.com/r/kelowna/) complain about bad traffic, especially on the Bennett bridge during rush hour, but it would cost an estimated $26 million per kilometre to widen Highway 97 to accommodate more traffic, and the principle of [induced demand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Induced_demand) has shown that traffic might still be bad after doing it.

That's what's happening right now, with our population of 590,000. What about in twenty years when economic growth brings 200,000 more people to the area? How can we build infrastructure to accommodate everyone?

## The solution

The answer is through densified transit—both by building new solutions and scaling up what we already have.

Part of the solution will need to be through buses, as they can transport far more people than cars can, and they can be easily and flexibly rerouted to serve changing population needs. But buses have considerable maintenance and labor costs, and tires create pollution as they degrade. Since we know the desire for intercommunity travel is only going to increase in coming years, we can set down roots along the connecting path and do one better.

Modern passenger rail is cheaper to operate in the long run, and rails don't pollute the environment the way tires do. At peak capacity, trains can also hold an estimated six times as many people as buses can, which means the potential of reducing congestion by six times as much.

Buses and trains are also much safer than cars. With cars, a momentary lapse in attention can lead to injury or death, but distracted or sleepy passengers will be transported to their destination safely on a bus or train. Because trains stay on their rails, they also resist inclement weather; trains are much less likely to skid on an icy road than a car or bus.

Let's turn to our southern neighbors for a moment. [In the USA](https://turbli.com/blog/the-safest-transport-modes-ranked-by-statistics-from-10-years-of-data/), the number of car fatalities over 10 years was over **120,000!** Over that same period, the number of bus fatalities was less than 500, and the number of train fatalities was **less than 60.** We can learn from their mistakes here!

## The plan

Modern trains run on electricity rather than fossil fuels, which is better for everyone's air quality and reduces the risk of wildfires. There are a number of options for how communities can source this electricity.

As one example, Dr. Gord Lovegrove at the University of British Columbia (UBC) [published an article in 2024](https://www.mdpi.com/2071-1050/16/7/3042) showing the viability of hydrogen rail (hydrail) in the Okanagan Valley. Hydrail is already [used to power trains in Germany](https://www.alstom.com/solutions/rolling-stock/alstom-coradia-ilint-worlds-1st-hydrogen-powered-passenger-train), and it would be less weight than an electric battery to transport up and down the mountainous terrain in BC's Interior, so we know this technology is a viable path forward. A properly funded planning study for the Okanagan might reveal other more efficient paths forward as well, so that's what we're working on first.

## Next steps

There are still a lot of questions we need to answer. Funding, routing, and related issues are at the top of people's minds whenever this subject comes up. But we believe these questions are worth answering, and we believe that an honest and fair process of answering them will lead to an end result that benefits everyone.

One thing we can do as members of the public is find ways to show grassroots public support. This will help summon the political will to make a change in all of the levels of government that need to be on board for this to happen. By connecting technical experts with political leaders, we have the potential to make something truly wonderful for this generation and the next!

## In conclusion

Nobody likes getting stuck in traffic, and communities around the world have shown that it doesn't have to be that way.

We know that better public transit means fewer car accidents and less strain on healthcare resources. It means better air quality and lower health risks in the long term.

More efficient transit means more people will want to take transit rather than drive, which makes driving conditions nicer for the remaining drivers (less traffic).

Better transit can improve economic growth and decrease homelessness by connecting people with jobs, especially across different cities. Transit connects people with businesses, which increases sales and keeps mom and pop shops in business, as well as providing people with the goods and services they need for their day-to-day lives.

Transit connects people with people, which increases community resilience and cultural opportunities—it makes people happier!

It is both our belief and our scientifically supported notion that a hydrail tram-train would improve all of these things in our community. The best time to plant a tree was a hundred years ago, but the second best time is today!
